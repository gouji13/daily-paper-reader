# Notes to Self: Can LLMs Benefit from Experiential Abstractions? 

Chang Liu, Xinyu Li, Artur Dubrawski 

Auton Lab, Carnegie Mellon University 

{changl8,xinyul2,awd}@cs.cmu.edu 

Abstract 

Humans distill experience into reusable ab-stractions , e.g., strategies and cautionary reminders, and apply them to gradually solve problems more effectively. We study whether Large Language Models (LLMs) can similarly benefit from such experien-tial abstractions. From LLMs’ solution traces on the MATH training set, a stronger teacher or the LLMs themselves extract natural-language abstractions into a retriev-able library. We explore two usage modes: (1) inference-time retrieval and (2) rein-forcement learning (RL) with abstraction-augmented training prompts. Experiential abstractions improve LLM performance on mathematical and logical reasoning bench-marks. Self-extracted abstractions match teacher-extracted ones, and our abstrac-tion usage framework can transfer to other datasets and models. These findings sug-gest LLMs can extract and apply experien-tial abstractions much as humans leverage distilled experience. 

1 Introduction 

Humans actively filter, compress, and internal-ize past experiences into reusable abstractions ,e.g., heuristics, strategies, and cautionary re-minders (Zsambok and Klein, 2014), rather than solving every new problem from scratch or merely accumulating memory: a student compiles a cheatsheet of recurring tricks; a tech-nician carries a mental catalog of techniques. These experiential abstractions act as cognitive toolkits for increasingly complex tasks (Gick and Holyoak, 1983; Polya, 1945). As large lan-guage models (LLMs) advance on complex rea-soning tasks such as mathematics (Shao et al., 2024), we ask an analogous question: can they similarly benefit from experiential abstractions derived from their own reasoning experience? Recent work explores experiential learning in LLMs (Zhao et al., 2024; Suzgun et al., 2026; Didolkar et al., 2025; Qu et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2026), but typically relies on large frontier mod-els as the target or as a teacher supervising smaller students, leaving unclear whether stu-dent LLMs can themselves extract or use such abstractions. We assess the opportunity of bridging this gap by deriving a library of experiential ab-stractions from a target LLM’s own training traces, with extraction by either a stronger teacher model or the target LLM itself. We study two modes of abstraction use (Fig-ure 1): (1) inference-time retrieval into the test prompt, and (2) RL fine-tuning with abstraction-augmented training prompts. We find that experiential abstractions can improve LLM mathematical reasoning abilities on MATH-500 (Lightman et al., 2024) for both modes, with consistent gains from abstraction-augmented RL post-training. Moreover, self-extracted abstractions yield gains compara-ble to teacher-extracted ones; our abstraction-usage pipeline can transfer to other datasets and domains (e.g., logical reasoning); and ab-stractions from one model’s traces can ben-efit a different target. These results suggest that LLMs can effectively extract and apply experiential abstractions from their own rea-soning experience, incrementally self-improving their abilities as their use expands. The code of this paper is available at https://github. com/ChangLiu-DrPatient/Notes-to-self .

3 Methods 

We denote the target LLM by πθ with param-eters θ, its solution trace on a question q by 

y ∼ πθ(· | q), and the abstraction library by 

L = {a1, . . . , a N }, where each ai is a natural-language abstraction. A sentence embedder 

ϕ(·) maps both questions and abstractions to a shared vector space for retrieval. We use outcome reward r(y, y ∗) ∈ { 0, 1}, set to one when the final answer in y matches the ground truth y∗. Figure 1 summarizes the full pipeline: we build a library of experiential abstractions from a target LLM’s training traces and study two modes of using it. 

3.1 Building the library of experiential abstractions 

We first collect solution traces of the tar-get LLM on a training set, using the 

MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) training split of 7,500 problems and sampling one trace per problem from πθ. Each trace is labeled correct or incorrect by comparing its final answer with the ground truth. We then prompt an extractor, either the target LLM itself or a stronger teacher, to sum-marize each trace into one or two reusable ab-stractions. Given the problem, the solution trace, the ground-truth answer, and the cor-rectness label, the extractor is instructed to produce single-sentence, reusable abstractions that are free of problem-specific numbers, vari-able names, or the answer itself. Extraction is conditioned on correctness: for correctly solved problems the extractor iden-tifies key reasoning moves worth reusing, pro-ducing strategy abstractions, while for failed problems it identifies mistake patterns to avoid, producing caution abstractions. We show the full extraction prompts in Appendix F.1. To construct the library, we embed each ab-straction with the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence encoder (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019) into 384-dimensional ℓ2-normalized vectors. We re-move near-duplicates by clustering abstractions with cosine-similarity union-find at a threshold of 0.85 , keeping strategy and caution entries in separate buckets, and storing the surviving 2entries. This deduplication scheme effectively reduces the library size by approximately 30%, as shown in Table 8 in Appendix C.

3.3 Abstraction-augmented RL post-training 

Here, abstractions enter during RL fine-tuning rather than only at inference. We apply the above retrieval procedure to each training ques-tion q, obtaining its abstraction set Rq, and fine-tune the target LLM with GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) so that the policy conditions on the pair (q, Rq) rather than on q alone. 5 Conclusion 

We studied whether LLMs benefit from ex-periential abstractions distilled from training traces, via either inference-time retrieval or abstraction-augmented RL post-training. Such abstractions extend model capability across mathematical and logical reasoning bench-marks. Moreover, small student LLMs extract abstractions with comparable effectiveness as frontier teachers, and abstractions can transfer across these target models. These findings in-dicate that experiential abstraction is a viable self-improvement signal for LLMs, mirroring how humans distill and reuse past experience. 4Limitations 

Our results further depend on several fixed design choices: a particular teacher model, sen-tence encoder, retrieval cutoff k, and dedupli-cation threshold, for which we do not provide a sensitivity analysis. Broader cross-model transfer claims would require additional pairs. Finally, RL post-training is conducted under a modest compute budget (one epoch, eight rollouts per prompt), and scaling the training could change the magnitude or ordering of the observed gains. The method targets mathematical problem solving and extends to logical reasoning, and we do not foresee direct societal risks. The main caveat is that an abstraction library can encode incorrect or misleading heuristics; outcome-reward RL mitigates this by reinforcing only verified-correct solutions.

6A Interpreting the effect of RL and abstractions 

In the main text (Section 4) we observe that GRPO train+test consistently underper-forms GRPO train : re-injecting abstractions at test time hurts a model already fine-tuned with them. We attribute this to the interaction be-tween abstractions and RL, and here we test two competing explanations. Under an in-ternalization hypothesis, the fine-tuned model has already absorbed the abstractions and is merely distracted by their re-injection. Under an exploration-vs-sharpening account, abstrac-tions perturb the output distribution to aid exploration, while RL sharpens that distribu-tion toward near-deterministic correct modes, so perturbing an already-tuned model displaces probability mass away from correct solutions. 

The internalization hypothesis does not hold. We evaluate three checkpoints—the base model (Baseline), plain GRPO (trained without abstractions), and GRPO train —each with and without abstractions injected at test time (Table 5). Internalization predicts that test-time injection should harm only GRPO train , the sole checkpoint exposed to ab-stractions during training. Instead, injection also degrades plain GRPO, which never encoun-tered abstractions in training and therefore cannot have internalized them. Internalization alone thus cannot explain the drop.

answers falls for Llama (7.3% → 4.2% ) but rises for Qwen (3.8% → 4.6% ). This disruption alone accounts for Qwen ’s most-degraded problems. For instance, on Qwen ’s single most-degraded problem (id 1332), 24 /32 

Inference abs samples derive the correct answer but omit the boxed format and are scored incorrect (Baseline: 11 /32 correct); no cautions are retrieved and the chain of thought remains intact, isolating the failure to formatting. 

Retrieval fit. Degradation also arises when retrieval surfaces abstractions that fit the query poorly. On a problem asking for the least pos-sible sum of distinct positive integers with a fixed product (id 203), Llama retrieves an apt minimum-sum strategy (“distribute the prime factors so that the resulting numbers are as close in magnitude...”) and adopts it near-verbatim, rising from 22% to 63% . Qwen in-stead retrieves a strategy for the opposite objec-tive (“To maximize the sum of distinct positive integers with a fixed product...”), and its fail-ing samples follow that prescription, dropping from 50% to 16% .Together, these analyses indicate that Qwen ’s degradation under inference-time injection stems from disrupted answer formatting and occasional poorly-matched retrievals, not from cautions functioning as harmful constraints.
