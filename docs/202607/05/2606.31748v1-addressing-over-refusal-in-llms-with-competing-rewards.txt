# Abstract 

Safety training on language models often induces over-refusal : improved safety on harmful prompts at the cost of increased refusal on harmless ones. Though this trade-off can be mitigated by training models with re-inforcement learning (RL) to reason before answering, it does not remove the underlying problem that reasoning can often be a “rubber stamp” for a predetermined response. In this paper, we address the safety-refusal trade-off by rethinking how models are trained to reason about safety. Our key insight is that unsafe reasoning can itself serve as a useful exploratory signal. Rather than preemptively blocking harmful thoughts, we encour-age the model to sufficiently explore unsafe reasoning but produce a safe response. The harmful exploration improves the model’s ability to distin-guish harmful from harmless prompts by resolving ambiguity, allowing it to remain safe while complying only when appropriate. We cast this as an adversarial optimization problem in which a reasoning player explores strategies for producing an unsafe response and an answer player ensures that the final output is safe. We train a single model with dense rewards to play both roles within one chain-of-thought, across different segments. To achieve this, we find that process rewards are crucial for stable optimization of competing objectives. Our resulting model SEAR deliberately engages in harmful reasoning as exploration while reliably flipping back to a safe answer. We demonstrate that this behavior helps mitigate over-refusal and defend against attacks that directly manipulate the reasoning to be harmful. 

# 1 Introduction 

Post-training large language models (LLMs) for safety is essential for reliable deployment since it reduces the risk of harmful outputs on unsafe or adversarial prompts (Kim et al., 2025a; Guan et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025e; Feng et al., 2025; Mou et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025d; Si et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). However, existing safety-training methods often induce over-refusal , making models less helpful on harmless prompts as they become safer against jailbreaks and malicious requests (Cui et al., 2024). How can we improve the safety of language models without unnecessarily refusing benign requests? A growing body of both open-source (Kim et al., 2025a;b; Zhang et al., 2025b) and propri-etary (Guan et al., 2025) work has proposed training models with reinforcement learning (RL) to reason explicitly about the malicious intent of an input prompt. The hope is that, rather than automatically refusing, a model can deliberate over the prompt, distinguish harmful from harmless intent, and respond more selectively. In practice, however, these methods only partially reduce over-refusal. As such, our analysis in §3 suggests that, in current open-source safety-trained models (Yang et al., 2025a; Olmo et al., 2025; Agarwal et al., 2025), reasoning often acts as a “rubber stamp” for the final decision rather than a mechanism for resolving ambiguity or addressing safety in a genuine manner. These models tend to follow one of two brittle behaviors: they either avoid unsafe interpretations altogether and default to refusal, or they explore unsafe possibilities but fail to recover to a safe final answer (Zhang et al., 2025c;d; Zhou et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025). To address this limitation, safety reasoning must support ambiguity resolution , not merely predict whether to refuse. Many ambiguous prompts admit both harmful and benign inter-1

Conversely, a model that explores such interpretations without reliable control may become vulnerable to jailbreaks or unsafe compliance. This limitation is especially striking in contrast to math and coding domains, where reasoning helps because it enables exploration and recovery : models can pursue an incorrect line of thought, recognize the mistake, and then self-correct their generation (Setlur et al., 2025). We argue that safety reasoning requires an analogous capability. A model should be able to examine safety-relevant interpretations of a prompt, including potentially unsafe ones, while still resolving the final answer safely. For clearly harmful prompts, this means identi-fying the unsafe interpretation and refusing or redirecting appropriately. For ambiguous prompts, it means finding benign interpretations that allow the model to remain helpful rather than defaulting to refusal. To instill this behavior into language models, we propose an RL approach for safety training based on competing rewards in §4. Standard outcome-reward RL (Kim et al., 2025a) typically assigns a single reward based on the correctness—safety in our setting—of the entire trajectory, using the same signal for both the reasoning process and the final response. In contrast, we assign separate rewards to the reasoning and answer segments of the trace, which we refer to as dense process rewards (Figure 1). Crucially, these rewards are competing: the model is rewarded for exploring increasingly harmful reasoning strategies while separately being rewarded for producing a safe final answer conditioned on those harmful segments. This encourages better exploration through isolated reasoning while still allowing the reasoning to inform the final response in one consecutive chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022), rather than constraining the reasoning based on the eventual answer. Through careful experiments, we identify two core design principles for training with competing rewards. First, in §4.2, we show that a mismatch in optimization between the competing objectives results in unstable behavior. Second, in §4.3, we show that dense outcome-level rewards alone are insufficient for learning these opposing objectives within a single model. Instead, in §4.4, we show that segment-level rewards on sampled rollouts, which we refer to as process-based rewards , are needed for proper credit assignment. These rewards teach the model both to explore safety-relevant interpretations and to recover to a safe final response within a single reasoning trajectory. Successfully training with competing rewards yields a model that reasons differently from standard safety-trained baselines: rather than preemptively shutting down risky chains of thought, it learns to reason through them while still producing safe answers, leading to less over-refusal. Using these principles, we train SEAR -1.5B, a model trained for selective safety through competing-reward reasoning. We make several observations in §6. First, SEAR achieves the best safety-refusal trade-off among a range of open models within its model class and size, outperforming the closest approach, TARS (Kim et al., 2025a), on this measurement. Second, despite having only 1.5B parameters, SEAR performs comparably to the much larger 2Preprint. Under review. TARS-7B and is more robust to adversarial attacks. Third, a model trained to examine unsafe interpretations is more robust against attacks that directly target reasoning, such as pre-filling. This capability is important for defending against stronger attacks that attempt to steer the model’s reasoning toward harmful conclusions. Overall, our results suggest that the safety-refusal trade-off is not only about deciding whether to refuse, but about learning 

how to resolve ambiguity : models must be able to reason through unsafe possibilities without allowing those possibilities to dictate the final response. 

# 2 Related Work 

Over-refusal, reasoning, and RL. Safety-trained models exhibit over-refusal , where refusal behavior generalizes beyond harmful prompts and suppresses compliance on harmless ones. This is typically evaluated using benchmarks of benign but ambiguous prompts that may appear unsafe to the model (R ¨ottger et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025a; Knight et al., 2025). Recent work has sought to mitigate over-refusal by leveraging reasoning, with the goal of helping models distinguish harmful from harmless intent more selectively. Some approaches adapt online RL to safety reasoning through reward design and context distillation (Kim et al., 2025a; Guan et al., 2025), while others use supervised fine-tuning or DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) to distill curated reasoning traces (Wang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025e; Feng et al., 2025; Mou et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025d; Si et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). These methods show that reasoning can reduce some unnecessary refusals, but they do not explicitly train the model to explore unsafe possibilities and then recover as in our work. In fact, we benchmark the model of Kim et al. (2025a) and find that it suffers from more over-refusal than our approach. Our approach treats harmful reasoning itself as a useful pathway for exploration, and uses process-based rewards to train both exploration and flip-back behavior. 

Training with competing objectives. Our work aligns with the literature on competing or adversarial objectives, often through two-player formulations, to improve exploration and robustness in reasoning tasks (Kirchner et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2021). Related ideas have also appeared in safety where models are trained to play attacker-defender roles (Liu et al., 2025b; Paulus et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2026; Tan et al., 2026), or to recover from harmful reasoning prefixes through supervised training (Kim et al., 2025b; Peng et al., 2025). Our approach is closer to the latter approach in that we incentivize reasoning to be unsafe, but differs in a crucial respect: prior work only trains to reactively recover from unintended harmful reasoning, whereas we explicitly train a single model to both actively enter harmful reasoning as a useful exploratory strategy and revert to a safe answer. Furthermore, we utilize dense process-based rewards to learn these opposing behaviors within one model, which we find to be crucial for optimization. 

# 3 Does Reasoning Help (with Exploration) in Safety? 

Reasoning helps solve long-form math problems because it supports exploration through test-time compute (Setlur* et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2026; Qu et al., 2025). A model may generate an incorrect intermediate step, recognize the mistake, and recover to a reasoning that leads to a correct answer. This ability to make mistakes without being dictated by them is a useful form of exploration. It allows the model to consider uncertain directions while still returning to a better final answer that is correct. 

What is the analogous behavior in safety? Ideally, when faced with a harmful or ambiguous prompt, a model should not default to refusal merely because one possible interpretation is unsafe. Instead, it should be able to reason through multiple possible reasoning traces on a given prompt, identify harmful intent, and produce a safe final response that is also as helpful as possible. Such behavior is especially important on ambiguous prompts (R ¨ottger et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025a; Knight et al., 2025), where avoiding all safety-relevant reasoning can lead to unnecessary refusal, while uncontrolled exploration can lead to unsafe compliance on harmful prompts. We care primarily about the safety and helpfulness of the final answer ; potentially harmful reasoning is useful only insofar as it serves as exploration that helps resolve ambiguity and arrive at the correct response. 3Preprint. # 4 Addressing Over-Refusal via RL with Competing Objectives 

The results above suggest that standard approaches for safety training do not produce diverse, exploratory reasoning traces when required to think harmfully. We posit that this issue stems from the insufficiency of outcome-based RL because a single scalar reward on the full trajectory lacks the expressive power to distinguish harmful from harmless reasoning steps when the final response is similar. In this section, we instead introduce 

competing rewards , which separately reward harmful exploration in the reasoning trace and safety in the final answer. This gives the model an explicit training signal for trajectories that first consider unsafe possibilities but then produce a safe final response when needed. §4.1 builds the objective for competing rewards, §4.2 and §4.3 show the instability of training with dense outcome-based rewards, and §4.4 shows the importance of process rewards for stabilizing training. 

4.1 Framework of Competing Objectives 

We first present an idealized formulation of our approach and then discuss the implementa-tion in the next section. Let πθ (τ | x) denote the LLM policy induced by parameters θ over a full trace τ given prompt x ∈ D h coming from a dataset of harmful prompts Dh, where the trajectory consists of both a reasoning segment and a final answer segment: τ = ( z, y), with 

z denoting the reasoning trace and y the final answer. We consider two reward functions: a reasoning reward rh(z), which measures how harmful or unsafe the reasoning segment is, and a final-answer reward rs(y), which measures how safe the final answer is. As we discuss in §4.2, rh and rs are derived from the same underlying safety reward model, with 

rh(·) = 1 − rs(·). We also consider another set of harmless prompts Di, where the goal is to optimize for some notion of a helpfulness reward ri (e.g., Di could be a set of math problems with ri measuring correctness of the final answer). 

Training goal. Our goal is to train the LLM to optimize these two opposing objectives 

in different parts of the same rollout on harmful prompts Dh. At a high level, we would like the policy to assign high probability to trajectories in which the reasoning segment attains a high harmfulness reward, while the final answer attains a high safety reward. Optimizing for such opposing rewards in consecutive segments encourages the model to learn a flip-back behavior after sufficient exploratory reasoning. This can help the model reduce over-refusal by outputting a safe answer only when necessary. In addition, we want the LLM to also optimize some notion of helpfulness reward ri on Di. We choose to optimize the LLM π on a mixture of both harmful and harmless prompts following Kim et al. (2025a) to prevent collapse into strict refusals as a safe answer. 4.2 Dense Outcome-Based RL

4.3 Dense Outcome-Based RL with Filtering Experiment. To resolve the imbalance of reasoning and answer optimization, during the gradient update, we filter out rollouts that already receive maximum answer safety during training. Hence, we update the model only on rollouts where answer safety is not yet fully optimized to match the speed in training for competing objectives. 

Results. Figure 3(b) shows that with such filtering, the reasoning harmfulness actually decreases over training, opposite of the imposed competing objectives. This is because base models have a strong correlation between reasoning and answer safety as shown in Figure 2 and the model is optimizing solely for answer safety. Hence, in this setting, the model has difficulty optimizing competing objectives for both answer safety rs and reasoning harmfulness rh. In conclusion, even after resolving the issue of saturation speed for the competing objectives, outcome-level based rewards still cannot perfectly optimize. 

4.4 Process Rewards 

Based on the results in the previous section, the main limitation of dense outcome-level rewards is that a scalar signal, even if dense, does not give us enough flexibility to optimize each segment of the trace individually. On harmful prompts, our goal is to learn a coupled behavior: the model should generate harmful reasoning and then produce a safe answer conditioned on that reasoning. Yet outcome-level RL assigns a single scalar reward to the entire rollout, making credit assignment difficult. This is especially a problem in current base models because the high correlation between reasoning and answer safety make it difficult to optimize in opposing directions under competing rewards. Hence, competing objectives require more precise reward assignment along the rollout to resolve this limitation. 

Experiment. We perform precise reward assignment by using process rewards (Figure 1). Rather than rewarding the full rollout only at the end, we assign rewards to the reasoning and answer segments separately, according to their own reward functions. By aligning the reward with the intended function of each segment, process rewards make the competing-objective optimization substantially easier to learn. Compliance (y-axis). We evaluate with the StrongReject evaluator (Souly et al., 2024) and take the complement of the refusal score. 

Model averaging for producing the final checkpoint. As in two-player games and GAN training (Goodfellow et al., 2014), optimization with competing objectives can exhibit oscillatory dynamics. Intermediate checkpoints within a run may differ meaningfully in the reasoning strategies they encode (Mescheder et al., 2017; Daskalakis et al., 2017). As a result, checkpoint quality can vary both within a training run and across runs, especially since our evaluation samples from each checkpoint directly at test time. To reduce this variance, we take inspiration from iterate averaging in saddle-point optimization, where averaging is known to improve stability and convergence behavior (Golowich et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021). Concretely, we train four independent runs and average their weights to obtain a single final model. For fairness, we apply the same procedure to the baseline runs without competing objectives. This choice is also consistent with prior work on model averaging and model soups, which shows that weight averaging across related solutions can improve robustness/accuracy without increasing inference cost (Wortsman et al., 2022). For a fair comparison, we also run model averaging for our dense/non-dense outcome-based reward baselines.

# 7 Conclusion & Discussion 

We find that current safety-trained reasoning models lack the ability to explore potentially harmful reasoning paths in a controlled way, a capability that is important for mitigating over-refusal in safety settings. To address this, we formulate an RL training procedure with competing rewards within a single generated rollout: the model is encouraged to explore harmful reasoning when useful while still being rewarded for producing a safe final answer. This induces behavior in which the model can first consider unsafe trajectories and then recover to a safe response. As a result, our approach mitigates over-refusal on ambiguous prompts without compromising safety. We also find that process-based rewards are crucial for optimizing such competing objectives with RL. Despite the efficacy of our approach, one limitation is the particular choice of dense process reward that we use. More generally, other forms of exploratory reasoning may prove more effective for mitigating over-refusal than directly exploring the most harmful path. Our dense-reward framework could be extended to such settings by designing alternative rewards for the reasoning segment. Nevertheless, we find that SEAR -1.5B, trained to actively explore harmful reasoning, achieves the lowest over-refusal among a wide range of models. We further present ablations showing that SEAR -1.5B has indeed learned flip-back behavior: a previously missing form of reasoning that is important for safety.

# B Attack Details 

We conduct two types of attacks: PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) and GCG (Zou et al., 2023). PAIR is a black-box attack that uses another attacker LLM to automate the search process for a harmful prompts. GCG is a white-box attack that uses token-level gradients of the loss with respect to the inputs to find candidate strings and selects the candidate that gives the lowest loss for a given generation target. We optimize for the beginning of the reasoning as the target for GCG. 

# C Evaluators and Judge 

On harmless prompts we use two reward models Skywork (Liu et al., 2025a) (Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B ), and an LLM judge, GPT-5-mini . Skywork encourages the model to output helpful answers to instruction tasks and GPT-5-mini encourages the model to not refuse to harmless prompts.
