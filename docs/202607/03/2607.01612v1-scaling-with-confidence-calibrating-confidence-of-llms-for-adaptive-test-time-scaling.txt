# Scaling with Confidence: Calibrating Confidence of LLMs for Adaptive Test Time Scaling 

Xuqing Yang 1, Yi Yuan 2, Shanzhe Lei 3, Xuhong Wang 3*1Shanghai Jiao Tong University, 2Southeast University, 3Shanghai AI Laboratory 

Abstract 

Training large language models (LLMs) with reinforcement learning (RL) has significantly advanced their performance on reasoning and question-answering tasks. However, prevailing RL reward designs typically prioritize response correctness, neglecting to incentivize models to express their confidence accurately. This leads to a critical problem: performance gains are of-ten accompanied by poor calibration between confidence and accuracy, misleading models to overconfidently hallucinate when uncertain. To address this limitation, we propose Correctness and Confidence Calibration Reinforcement 

Learning ( C3RL ), a novel RL algorithm in-tegrating correctness, calibration and dataset-informed reference accuracy rewards together. Comprehensive evaluation across 8 text and multimodal datasets demonstrates that C3RL enhances calibration without sacrificing accu-racy, outperforming the current state-of-the-art method in both performance and calibration metrics. Utilizing the well-calibrated verbal-ized confidence from C3RL, we further intro-duce Confidence-based Adaptive Test Time 

Scaling ( CAS ), an adjustable inference-time strategy that allocates computational resources based on response confidence. Experiments show that CAS surpasses majority voting on both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets while reducing the inference budget by up to 12.33 times. We believe the synergy of C3RL and CAS paves the way for deploying more re-liable and resource-efficient LLMs. The code, data and models will be released. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demon-strated remarkable performance in reasoning, ques-tion answering, and code generation (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023). However, their im-pressive capabilities are accompanied by a critical failure mode: when faced with uncertainty, LLMs 

> *Correspondence: wangxuhong@pjlab.org.cn

frequently produce overconfident but incorrect out-puts—a phenomenon widely referred to as halluci-nation (Ji et al., 2023). Such behavior is not merely an academic curiosity, but can lead to serious con-sequences, including misinformation propagation and decision failures in safety-critical domains. To mitigate these risks, it is desirable for LLMs not only to generate correct outputs but also to reliably express their epistemic uncertainty. This property, known as confidence calibration , refers to the ability of a model to produce probability estimates that faithfully reflect the likelihood of correctness (Guo et al., 2017). Well-calibrated con-fidence serves as a foundation to abstain from un-certain answering, defer to human oversight, or dy-namically adjust computational budget when confi-dence is low. Despite its importance, confidence calibration for LLMs remains underexplored and faces several limitations. First, they typically reduce calibration to a binary classification task (certain vs. uncer-tain), which is too coarse to capture the nuanced uncertainty of LLM predictions. Second, they of-ten improve calibration at the expense of accuracy, since models may learn to produce deliberately incorrect low-confidence answers to minimize cal-ibration loss. Moreover, they rarely explore the potential of calibrated confidence for downstream decision-making, such as adaptive inference and resource allocation (Zhao et al., 2024). 

Our work addresses these gaps by jointly op-timizing correctness and calibration within a re-inforcement learning framework and by exploit-ing calibrated confidence for adaptive test-time scaling. Specifically, we design an approach that explicitly teaches LLMs to signal low confidence when they are uncertain . Moreover, we leverage the resulting calibrated confidence to guide early stopping in test time scaling, adjusting more com-putation to low-confidence samples. To the best of our knowledge, our method is the first to inte-1

2 Related Work 

Recent studies reveal that, LLMs often exhibit over-confidence (Xiong et al., 2023; Mei et al., 2025) and hallucination (Zhang et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2024), which undermines their reliability. Exist-ing efforts to improve confidence estimation and calibration can be broadly grouped into two major lines: (i) prompt and sampling-based calibration and (ii) training and RL-based calibration. We further discuss related work on adaptive test time scaling. 

2.1 Prompt and Sampling based Calibration 

Prompt-based approaches guide LLMs to explicitly verbalize their confidence or self-assess their relia-bility after producing an answer. (Tian et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a) show that verbalized confidence is often poorly calibrated though calibration improves with model scale and reasoning capability. More recent reasoning-oriented studies (Mei et al., 2025; Yoon et al., 2025) demonstrate that introspective or “slow thinking” chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting can yield bet-ter alignment between confidence and correctness. However, these prompting-based techniques de-pend heavily on prompt wording, limiting robust-ness and scalability (Zhao et al., 2021). Another line of research estimates confidence im-plicitly from response consistency. These sampling-based approaches rely on response diversity or ma-jority voting (Kuhn et al., 2023; Aichberger et al., 2025; Kang et al., 2025), where agreement across multiple generations serves as uncertainty. While these methods correlate well with empirical accu-racy, they are computationally expensive. 

2.2 Training and RL based Calibration 

Training-based methods aim to learn calibrated confidence distributions through supervised or re-inforcement objectives. Lin et al. (2022) intro-duces dataset-level supervision by correlating re-sponse types with empirical accuracy, while R-Tuning (Zhang et al., 2023) augments supervised fine-tuning data with binary “I am sure/unsure” la-bels. Although effective for coarse uncertainty, such designs fail to capture fine-grained, context-dependent confidence. More principled formulations employ reinforce-ment learning (RL) to jointly optimize correct-ness and calibration. SaySelf (Xu et al., 2024) and Stangel et al. (2025) train LMs with proper scoring rules (e.g., Brier or log loss) as rewards, directly aligning verbalized confidence with out-put correctness. However, these objectives often prioritize calibration alone, potentially encourag-ing low-confidence incorrect answers to minimize loss. RLCR (Damani et al., 2025) incorporates cal-ibrated reward functions to balance accuracy and calibration, though applications remain limited to question-answering or Maths tasks. Instead, our model trained with C3RL on reasoning-intensive tasks maintains accuracy and reaches optimal im-provement in calibration. 

2.3 Adaptive Test Time Scaling 

Adaptive sampling has emerged as a powerful ap-proach to reduce the computational cost of test time scaling. Adaptive-Consistency (Aggarwal et al., 2023) introduces a model-agnostic technique that dynamically adjusts the number of samples per question using lightweight stopping criteria based on the frequency of answers. Li et al. (2023) takes an alternative approach by dividing the sampling process into sequential windows and stopping when all samples within a window are the same. 3 Method 

In this section, we introduce the construction pro-cess of training data (Section 3.1), the reward de-sign for C3RL (Section 3.2) and the adaptive test time scaling strategy based on calibrated confi-dence (Section 3.3). Figure 1 shows the pipeline of the whole framework. 

3.1 Training Dataset Construction 

Training LLMs via Reinforcement Learning re-quires comprehensive and moderately difficult datasets. We create our high-quality training datasets and assign an accuracy tag to each data. We first collect datasets in mathematics, sci-ence and logic reasoning. The selected datasets are denoted as D, with the following components: (1) Mathematics and Science: NuminaMath-TIR (Jia LI, 2024) (69k numerical-answer math problems targeting computation and reasoning) and WebInstruct-verified (Ma et al., 2025) (4.5k selected numerical science problems with integer answers); (2) Logical Reasoning: LogicNLI (Tian et al., 2021) (16k problems unbinding the target first-order logic reasoning from commonsense in-ference) and LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020) (8,678 QA problems focusing on deductive reasoning). (1,073 samples, 1%). For each question q in Dtrain , we use the base model (Qwen2.5VL-7B-Instruct (Team, 2025) or Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (Meta, 2024)) to generate 10 answers with a decoding temperature 0.7. 3.2 C3RL 

Previous RL training methods for calibration sim-ply incentivize calibration and pose potential risks that models learn to give incorrect answers de-liberately with low confidence. To prevent such a “calibrated but wrong" shortcut, we design a novel Correctness and Confidence Calibration Re-inforcement Learning ( C3RL ). For each question 3q ∈ D train , we prompt the base model to generate an answer aq with an associated verbalized confi-dence caq ∈ { 1, 2, ..., 10 }, where caq = 1 means the model is totally uncertain and caq = 10 means the model is totally certain. We train the model (same as the base model) utilizing the following reward: 

RC3RL (q, a q, c aq , T q) = Rcorrectness + Rcalibration 

+ Rreference 

The three parts in the reward are as follows: 

Correctness Reward: The traditional RLVR (Lambert et al., 2024) reward 

Rcorrectness (aq) = I(aq = a∗) ∈ { 0, 1}. This part ensures the model will update in the “correct” direction instead of “calibrated but wrong” way. 

Calibration Reward: We set a confidence threshold t=5 to classify the answer aq into “certain” (caq > t ) and “uncertain” ( caq ≤ t) categories. We encourage “certain correct” and “uncertain incor-rect” answers with Rcalibration = + β while pe-nalizing “certain incorrect” and “uncertain correct” responses with Rcalibration = −β. The ablation process to determine β is detailed in A.5. 

Reference Accuracy Reward: To utilize the prior accuracy information in data construction pro-cess, we customize a reward for question q based on reference accuracy tag Tq. When the model answers correctly from formerly “all incorrect" questions, we incentivize it with Rreference = + α.On the contrary, if the model hallucinates with incorrect answers from formerly “all correct" ques-tions, we punish it with Rreference = −α. This design forces the model to remain correct if it can originally give correct answers and further stops the model from falling into “calibrated but wrong" risks. The ablation process to select α is elaborated in A.5. 

3.3 Confidence-based Adaptive Test Time Scaling (CAS) 

Prior adaptive self-consistency methods typically rely on frequency or entropy to determine early-stopping criteria. However, such signals are of-ten uncalibrated and fail to accurately reflect the model’s true confidence. To address this limitation, we propose a Confidence-based Adaptive test-time Scaling (CAS) strategy, which integrates calibrated verbalized confidence into the stopping criterion. After generating each batch of responses, CAS ap-plies a well-calibrated stopping criterion based on the verbalized confidence of model outputs to de-cide whether to (1) continue sampling or; (2) halt and return the current highest confidence-weighted answer. 4.1.5 Analysis: C3RL Advantage 

The advantage of C3RL over RLCR in balancing performance and calibration is due to the following two reasons: (1) Reference accuracy information: The ablation study has demonstrated the effective-ness of reference accuracy reward in advancing calibration; (2) Importance of learning confidence threshold: RLCR, driven by the Brier Score (see A.2.3), tends to train LLMs to indicate totally con-fident ( c=10) or totally inconfident ( c=1) responses, which degrades to a binary case. On the contrary, C3RL guides the model to learn the confidence threshold t=5 and gives fine-grained confidence to distinguish between certainty and uncertainty. The difference in confidence distribution between C3RL and RLCR is demonstrated in A.4.

4.2.3 Analysis: Imbalanced Budget Allocation 

CAS adjusts the inference budget from high-confidence sampling traces to low-confidence traces with early-stopping strategy. To verify the rationality of this imbalanced mechanism, we com-pare the benefits from test time scaling for high-confidence and low-confidence samples. We use the C3RL model for generation and choose the three text benchmarks in Sec 4.2.1 for evaluation. We divide the samples into two sets: High-confidence Set and Low-confidence Set. For each question, if the CAS sampling time n ≤ 4

(which means CAS stops early because the final answer reaches high confidence quickly), we cate-gorize this question to High-confidence Set. Oth-erwise, we assign the sample to Low-confidence Set. We study how these two sets benefit differently from majority voting. As shown in Figure 2, when test time N in-creases to 64, the High-confidence Set remains unchanged in performance (<0.2%), while the Low-confidence Set jumps dramatically from 49.0% to 54.5%. The fact that the Low-confidence Set bene-fits much more from test time scaling than its coun-terpart rationalizes the CAS strategy of shifting limited computing resource from high-confidence samples to low-confidence samples. 

4.3 Can C3RL contribute to CAS? 

To explore the influence of calibrated confidence in test time scaling, we compare two models: The base model and the model trained with C3RL. The Confidence-weighted Majority Voting (CMV) method is also evaluated to display the contribution of calibrated confidence. As shown in Figure 3, C3RL+CAS outperforms all baseline methods when number of samples N is sufficiently large ( ≥8), demonstrating the scalable effectiveness of our approach. A key observation is the stratification of test-time scaling strategies in the C3RL-trained model. In comparison, the base model benefits little from CAS than AC. This stark contrast underscores that confidence calibra-tion through C3RL is prerequisite for CAS to un-lock its full potential. The performance leap from base+CAS to C3RL+CAS demonstrates their syner-gistic relationship: C3RL provides well-calibrated verbalized confidence, and CAS efficiently lever-ages it for test-time scaling. 

5 Conclusion 

This work presents Correctness and Confidence Calibration Reinforcement Learning (C3RL) to support downstream Confidence-based Adaptive test time Scaling (CAS). By integrating correct-ness, calibration and prior reference information together, C3RL achieves the best trade-off between performance and calibration, providing credible verbalized confidence. Based on well-calibrated confidence, CAS takes inference most efficiently among test time scaling methods by adjusting computing resources to low-confidence samples. Our results highlight a unified pipeline for reli-able LLMs and efficient inference under limited 8resources. 

Limitations 

There are several limitations for our C3RL-CAS framework. First, the high-quality training data in science domain is not sufficient compared to Maths and logic reasoning domains, which may result in training bias. Second, although C3RL achieves best accuracy-calibration trade-off, its calibration ability is still inferior to SaySelf, which means if we want to enhance the model accuracy, we have to sacri-fice calibration. Third, the advantage of CAS over Adaptive-Consistency in small generation cases (N <8) is not considerable, which requires more reasonable stopping criteria and better-calibrated confidence from C3RL.

A.1 Experimental Data A.1.1 Training Dataset Construction 

We modify the two reasoning datasets Webinstruct-verified (Ma et al., 2025) and Numina-NLI (Jia LI, 2024) to support regex check of response correct-ness. The Webinstruct-verified dataset is modified from the original 231,833 samples. For Numina-NLI and Webinstruct-verified datasets whose answers are numerical expressions, we check whether the generated samples are "math valid". We exclude the questions with no valid responses among 10 generations. For Logic-NLI (Tian et al., 2021) and LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020) datasets, we follow the selection process specified in C2RM (He et al., 2025). Finally, D composes of the following train-ing datasets: Numina-NLI (69,043), Webinstruct-verified (4,488), LogicNLI (16,000) and LogiQA (17,730). 

A.1.2 Benchmark Details 

We evaluate reasoning and logic capabilities across reasoning, math and logic benchmarks with text and multimodal datasets: 

Text: (1) AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023): 8269 problems from college admission tests, law school admission tests, math competitions, lawyer qualifi-cation tests, and national civil service exams. We use the combination of train, validation and test set for evaluation; (2) OlympiadBench (text) (He et al., 2024): Olympiad-level questions including problems from maths and physics competitions. A.10 Ablation Experiment Evaluating RLCR + Reference Accuracy Reward 

We additionally conduct an ablation experiment by incorporating the reference accuracy reward into RLCR, denoted as RLCR w.t. Ref . This experi-ment aims to verify whether the improvement of C3RL mainly comes from introducing additional reward terms or from the threshold-guided calibra-tion formulation itself. Table 12 reports the evaluation results on AGIEval and MMLU. Compared with RLCR, di-rectly adding the reference accuracy reward to RLCR does not improve calibration performance and even slightly degrades AUROC and ECE on both benchmarks. In contrast, C3RL w/o Ref con-sistently outperforms RLCR and RLCR w.t. ref in calibration metrics, while the full C3RL achieves the best overall accuracy-calibration trade-off. These results demonstrate that the improvement of C3RL does not simply arise from adding ex-tra reward signals. Instead, the threshold-guided calibration objective plays the dominant role in preventing confidence collapse and enabling more fine-grained uncertainty estimation.
