## Abstract 

Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) offer a promising alternative to reward-maximizing reinforcement learning (RL) for learning large reasoning models. GFlowNets encourage diverse reasoning paths by matching reward distributions rather than collapsing to dominant modes. Recent work shows promise on math and code benchmarks, but scaling GFlowNet-style RL to modern post-training pipelines remains difficult. In the regimes that matter for LLM post-training, model size, rollout horizon, reward noise, and distributed-systems complexity all increase together, and a learned prompt-conditional partition function can become a source of gradient instability and engineering overhead rather than a useful normalizer. We address these challenges through systematic analysis to reveal what compo-nents are truly necessary for GFlowNet-based RL at scale. Our main finding is that the learned partition function, previously treated as essential, can be replaced by an in-batch Monte Carlo estimate computed from the rollout group already required for training. Based on this observation, we propose GFlowRL , a stream-lined GFlowNet-style RL algorithm that removes the auxiliary partition network entirely while preserving the reward-distribution-matching objective. The recipe is completed by two stabilizers: importance-sampling correction for rollout/trainer drift, and asymmetric flow-gap clipping for outlier residuals. GFlowRL improves both stability and performance compared to prior work. It exceeds all counterparts on math, code, and adversarial red-teaming benchmarks, reaching a Codeforces rating of 2048 at the 14B scale (within 25 Elo of o3-mini) and attaining the highest average ASR@1 on AdvBench and HarmBench, outperforming the previous SOTA multi-turn attacker in a regime where FlowRL, a previous GFlowNet-style RL method, diverges. The same recipe transfers to all evaluated MoE configurations up to 235B parameters, where FlowRL again fails to converge. To our knowledge, 

GFlowRL is the first GFlowNet-style RL algorithm to scale stably across both dense and sparse architectures for large reasoning models. Code will be released at 

https://github.com/microsoft/gflowrl .

## 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become the defining post-training paradigm for the current gen-eration of large language models. The most capable reasoning systems deployed today, including OpenAI o1 [OpenAI, 2024] and o3 [OpenAI, 2025], DeepSeek-R1 [Guo et al., 2025a], and Gem-ini [Kavukcuoglu, 2025], all rely on RL as a central component of their post-training pipelines. The effectiveness of this stage directly determines model performance on high-value tasks such as mathematical reasoning, competitive programming, scientific problem solving, and agentic decision making. As models scale and reasoning chains grow longer, the choice of RL algorithms has become a first-order architectural decision. 

> Preprint. arXiv:2607.13394v1 [cs.CL] 15 Jul 2026

The dominant RL approaches, including PPO [Schulman et al., 2017], GRPO [Shao et al., 2024], OMD [Kimi-Team et al., 2025] and their variants, are fundamentally reward maximizing. They con-centrate probability mass on high reward modes, often collapsing solution diversity [Xu et al., 2025]. In chain-of-thought reasoning, where multiple valid solution paths are essential for generalization and robustness, this behavior introduces a structural limitation. Existing mitigations, such as entropy bonuses [Shen, 2026], adaptive clipping, and token-level exploration, treat diversity [Yu et al., 2025b] as a post hoc regularizer while leaving the core reward maximizing objective unchanged. Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) [Bengio et al., 2023, 2021, Hu et al., 2024] offer a fun-damentally different paradigm: instead of maximizing the expected reward, they learn policies that sample trajectories in proportion to their reward . When applied to LLM post-training, this distribution-matching perspective directly addresses mode collapse, encouraging coverage of all high-reward reasoning paths rather than concentrating on one. Recent approaches such as FOR [Yu et al., 2025a] and FlowRL [Zhu et al., 2026] are among the first to introduce this idea into LLM reasoning, adopting a trajectory balance objective with a learned approximation to the partition function Zϕ(x). In FlowRL, Zϕ(x) is parameterized as a three-layer MLP on top of the prompt’s final hidden state, whereas in FOR it is represented as a scalar shared across all prompts. 

The scaling problem. While FlowRL shows the promise of GFlowNet-style training, deploying it in the regimes that matter for modern LLM post-training remains challenging. Even on dense backbones, training must operate under a short optimization horizon, long reasoning rollouts, large pretrained policies, and tight distributed-systems budgets. In this setting, jointly training a new initialized partition model alongside the policy becomes increasingly brittle as scale grows: gradient spikes become more frequent, the auxiliary module adds optimizer and synchronization overhead, and the learned normalizer lags behind the much larger pretrained policy. MoE routing [Guo et al., 2025a, Achiam et al., 2023, Grattafiori et al., 2024, Kimi-Team et al., 2025, Kavukcuoglu, 2025] amplifies these issues further by introducing nondeterminism and implicit off-policy mismatch between sampling and training [Thinking Machines, 2025, Yao et al., 2025], but MoE is best viewed as the hardest stress test of a more general scaling bottleneck rather than the root cause itself. 

The root cause. We attribute these failures to a key design choice: the learnable partition function 

Zϕ. In LLM post-training, there is a fundamental asymmetry between the two components being optimized. The policy is initialized from a pretrained model with billions of well-trained parameters and requires only modest updates, whereas Zϕ is randomly initialized and must learn a complex quantity from scratch within the same limited training steps. As a result, log Zϕ behaves largely as a source of noise for much of training, causing the trajectory balance objective to be dominated by variance rather than the reward signal. Section 3.1 provides a detailed analysis of the gradient behavior and training dynamics. Empirically, we have observed two consistent patterns: (1) replacing Zϕ with a random noise yields comparable performance, suggesting that it contributes little to modeling; and (2) FlowRL exhibits gradient norms orders of magnitude larger than GRPO, leading to significant optimization instability without bringing any visible gains in accuracy. Based on this analysis, we propose GFlowRL , a streamlined GFlowNet-based RL algorithm that does not use the learned partition function. In its place, we use a simple yet effective in-batch Monte Carlo estimate of log Z, estimated directly from the rollout group already sampled during GRPO for each prompt. This simple change has three consequences: (i) the gradient norms remain the same scale as that of GRPO, restoring training stability; (ii) the auxiliary network and its optimizer state in distributed training are all removed; and (iii) the algorithm uses a nearly identical infrastructure of GRPO, while retaining the reward-distribution-matching semantics of trajectory balance. The recipe is completed by two stabilizers: importance-sampling correction for rollout/trainer drift, and asymmetric flow-gap clipping for outlier residuals. To our knowledge, GFlowRL is the first GFlowNet-based RL algorithm to scale stably across both dense and sparse architectures .In summary, our contributions are threefold: 1. We identify the use of the learned partition function Zϕ as a key source of instability in GFlowNet-based RL training for LLMs. We provide empirical evidence that Zϕ offers almost no benefit for post-training and instead can make optimization unnecessarily challenging. 2. We propose GFlowRL , where Zϕ is replaced with an in-batch Monte Carlo estimate, thus removing the auxiliary network and its associated distributed overhead while retaining the trajectory balance objective’s fixed point. 23. We demonstrate stable training across both dense and sparse MoE architectures, including regimes where FlowRL diverges. At the 14B dense scale, GFlowRL reaches 2048 Codeforces Elo, exceed-ing DeepCoder-14B by +112 , FlowRL-14B by +144 , and OpenAI’s o1 by +157 . On adversarial red-teaming, GFlowRL attains the highest average ASR@1 on AdvBench ( 82.5% ) and HarmBench (79.5% ), surpassing the prior SOTA multi-turn attacker in a noisy-reward regime where FlowRL diverges. Scaling to MoE, GFlowRL reaches 1999 Codeforces Elo on Qwen3-30B-A3B with only 3B active parameters, and the same recipe transfers to Qwen3-235B-A22B on math. 

## 2 Preliminaries 

We formulate chain-of-thought reasoning as a conditional generation task. Given a prompt 

x ∈ X (e.g., a math problem), the policy πθ (y | x) autoregressively generates a response 

y = ( y1, y 2, . . . , y T ) containing intermediate reasoning and a final answer. A verifier provides a scalar reward r(x, y) ∈ R. For example, the reward may be a binary correctness signal for math [Shao et al., 2024] or unit-test pass rates for code [Jain et al., 2025]. 

Policy notation. A symbol π(y | x) denotes a policy, i.e., a conditional distribution over responses 

y given prompt x. Following [Schulman et al., 2017, Shao et al., 2024, Yao et al., 2025], πθ is the trainable policy, πref the frozen reference (e.g., the pretrained model), and πold the rollout policy used to sample the current group; πold is a snapshot of πθ used for rollout generation while πθ is updated against those rollouts.

## 3 Methodology 

3.1 Why the Learned Partition Function Fails 

While the TB objective is theoretically principled, we argue that its standard instantiation is poorly suited to the LLM post-training regime. The crux is a learning-horizon mismatch between the two components of the joint optimization. 

The learning-horizon mismatch. The policy πθ is initialized from a pretrained LLM checkpoint with billions of parameters that encode rich linguistic and mathematical priors from trillions of tokens of pretraining [Qwen-Team, 2024]. Post-training RL needs only to refine this strong prior, and 3typically runs for a few hundred gradient updates. The partition function Zϕ, in contrast, is a freshly initialized network (e.g., 3-layer MLP as in FlowRL) that must learn a complex, prompt-conditional quantity from scratch within the same short window. This asymmetry means that throughout most of training, log Zϕ is effectively a random function of the prompt. 

Empirical evidence I: the partition function contributes little. If the partition function Zϕ provides a meaningful learning signal, replacing it with noise should degrade performance. We test this by removing Zϕ entirely and substituting it with random samples drawn from a normal distribution with mean μ = 0 .5 and standard deviation σ = 1 . Detailed hyperparameters are listed in Table 9. Surprisingly, the performance remains unchanged and even slightly improves: the random variant achieves 36 .19% average accuracy versus 35 .61% for the learned partition function. This indicates that Zϕ does not encode useful information for the policy and is effectively ignored during training. This instability arises from the unbounded contribution of log Zϕ(x), in training when the network is poorly calibrated, so policy updates are dominated by high-variance signals unrelated to the reward or likelihood. Together, these results reveal a clear pattern: the partition function neither improves performance nor behaves benignly. It acts as a source of noise that degrades optimization stability without contributing a useful learning signal, motivating a simple question: if the learned partition function is both unnecessary and harmful, can we replace it with a non-learned estimator while preserving the distribution-matching objective?

Our proposal is to replace the learned network log Zϕ(x) with an in-batch Monte Carlo estimate computed from the rollouts themselves. Why this works. When the flow-gap clip is inactive ( ˜g(i) = g(i)), the residual inside the square in Eq. 8 is exactly ∆(i)(θ). Thus, without length normalization, the objective preserves the same target fixed point as trajectory balance: any zero-loss self-consistent fixed point satisfies πθ (y |

x) ∝ πθref (y | x) exp( βr (x, y)) (Proposition B.1, Appendix B). This result is a characterization of the desired fixed point, not a guarantee that the finite-batch nonconvex optimization dynamics necessarily converges to it. The clip is a safeguard on outlier rollouts: at the fixed point the flow gap is zero, so the clip is inactive in a neighborhood of the optimum and does not perturb the stationary distribution (Remark B.3). Length normalization introduces a mild approximation whose effect vanishes when response lengths are approximately uniform across rollouts, which holds in practice for reasoning tasks (Remark B.4). The key practical difference is that Zt(x) is an estimator whose scale is automatically commensurate with r/β and log πθ , eliminating the gradient explosion observed with the learned Zϕ. Moreover, Zt(x) requires no additional parameters, no separate learning rate, and no cross-module synchronization in distributed training. 

## 4 Experiment 

4.1 Experimental Setup Models, datasets, and metrics. We evaluate GFlowRL across three reasoning domains. For math , we train Qwen2.5-7B/32B [Qwen-Team, 2024] on six standard benchmarks (AIME2024/2025 [Zhang and Math-AI, 2024, 2025], AMC2023 [Zhang and Math-AI, 2023], MATH-500 [Lightman et al., 2023], Minerva [Lewkowycz et al., 2022], Olympiad [He et al., 2024]), reporting Avg@16 [Yu et al., 52025b, Zhu et al., 2026]. For coding , we train Deepseek-R1-Distilled-Qwen-7B/14B [Guo et al., 2025a] on LiveCodeBench [Jain et al., 2025], Codeforces [Penedo et al., 2025] (Rating/Percentile, following [Luo et al., 2025]), and HumanEval+ [Chen et al., 2021]. For LLM safety , we use SEMA [Feng et al., 2026] on AdvBench [Zou et al., 2023] and HarmBench [Mazeika et al., 2024], reporting ASR@1. MoE scaling uses Qwen3-30B-A3B and Qwen3-235B-A22B. 

Baselines and implementation. We compare against FlowRL [Zhu et al., 2026] as the distribution-matching baseline, and PPO [Schulman et al., 2017], GRPO/GRPO+ [Shao et al., 2024, Luo et al., 2025], and REINFORCE++ [Hu et al., 2025] as reward-maximization baselines. Our PyTorch imple-mentation builds on verl , deepcoder , FlowRL , and SEMA , using their default training configurations unless otherwise noted. Detailed information such as dataset and framework links are provided in Appendix D.

GFlowRL and FlowRL substantially improve over PPO and GRPO on both math and code. On 7B math, GFlowRL exceeds GRPO by +8 .44 average points (40.92 vs. 32.48); on Codeforces, both GFlowNet-style methods open a 200–330 Elo advantage over GRPO. On every benchmark where both converge, GFlowRL also exceeds FlowRL: best 7B math average ( 40.92 , +5 .29 ), highest LiveCodeBench Avg@16 ( 38.62 , +1 .19 ), highest Codeforces rating ( 1646 , +97 ) and percentile (88.0% , +4 .7% ), and highest HumanEval+ score ( 84.93 , +1 .65 ). Combined with the diagnostic in Sec. 4.2, this confirms that GFlowRL delivers the benefits of TB with an efficient and stable estimator. 

GFlowRL succeeds where FlowRL fails to converge. The strongest evidence for the streamlined design comes from adversarial red-teaming, where the reward signal is noisier and sparser than in math or code. On AdvBench and HarmBench (Table 4), FlowRL fails to converge and produces no usable attacker, while GFlowRL trains stably and attains the highest average ASR@1 on both benchmarks (82.5% on AdvBench, 79.5% on HarmBench), surpassing the SOTA multi-turn attacker SEMA [Feng et al., 2026] by +2 .4 and +4 .5 points respectively. Removing the high-variance log Zϕ term restores stable optimization, turning parity on math/code into a strict capability advantage: GFlowRL covers the union of tasks where either GFlowNet-style or GRPO/PPO training works.

## 5 Related Work 

RL for LLM reasoning. RLHF established post-training with PPO-style optimization and a reference-policy regularizer [Ouyang et al., 2022, Schulman et al., 2017]. Recent reasoning systems shift to verifiable rewards on math and code, enabling critic-free or group-based optimization at scale (GRPO, REINFORCE++, DAPO, DeepSeek-R1, Kimi k1.5, DeepCoder) [Shao et al., 2024, Hu et al., 2025, Yu et al., 2025b, Guo et al., 2025a, Kimi-Team et al., 2025, Luo et al., 2025]. These methods maximize expected reward and tend to concentrate mass on a small set of dominant trajectories. 

GFlowNets and distribution matching. GFlowNets sample compositional objects in proportion to reward rather than greedily maximizing it [Bengio et al., 2021, 2023, Malkin et al., 2022]. The closest prior work for LLM post-training is FOR and FlowRL [Yu et al., 2025a, Zhu et al., 2026], both of which instantiate the trajectory balance objective with a learned prompt-conditional partition network. GFlowRL stays in this distribution-matching line but replaces that auxiliary network with a rollout-based estimate, motivated by its instability when jointly trained with a frontier-scale policy. 

In-batch estimation and variance reduction. Our estimator connects to a long line of variance-reduced score-function estimators, from REINFORCE baselines [Williams, 1992] to leave-one-out and control-variate methods [Richter et al., 2020, Grathwohl et al., 2018], and in LLM RL to group-relative normalization in GRPO and REINFORCE++ [Shao et al., 2024, Hu et al., 2025]. We use the rollout group to estimate the partition term implied by the distribution-matching objective and inject it as a stop-gradient baseline, structurally analogous to a GRPO group-mean baseline but applied to the trajectory-balance residual rather than the advantage. ## 6 Conclusion 

Distribution-matching RL is appealing for LLM reasoning because it preserves multiple high-reward solution paths, but our results suggest the standard GFlowNet recipe does not transfer cleanly to modern post-training. The bottleneck is not the trajectory-balance objective itself but the learned partition function: in the short-horizon regime of LLM RL, a randomly initialized Zϕ must be learned from scratch alongside a large pretrained policy that only needs modest refinement, turning the partition term into a source of noise, gradient instability, and systems overhead. We proposed 

GFlowRL , which replaces the learned partition network with an in-batch Monte Carlo estimate from the rollout group already required for training, combined with importance sampling and flow-gap clipping for stability. The result is a simpler algorithm with a GRPO-compatible pipeline that retains the reward-distribution-matching perspective: it matches or improves on FlowRL where FlowRL converges, restores convergence in noisier settings such as red-teaming, and scales cleanly to large MoE backbones. The broader takeaway is that scaling GFlowNet-style RL to large reasoning models may depend less on adding auxiliary machinery and more on identifying which parts of the original objective are unnecessary in this regime. 97 Acknowledgment

GFlowRL ’s in-batch Monte Carlo estimate of log Z can have higher variance in principle, especially when the group size is small. In practice, our two stabilizers, importance-sampling correction and flow-gap clipping, help control this variance, and the resulting streamlined estimator consistently outperforms the learned alternative across our evaluated settings. Nevertheless, a more precise characterization of the bias-variance tradeoff, as well as lower-variance group-based estimators, remains an important direction for future work. Another limitation is generality. Our experiments cover math reasoning, code reasoning, and red-teaming for language models, but it remains unclear whether the same estimator-centric design principle extends to broader agentic or multimodal RL settings. We leave this broader validation to future work.

## C Extended Related Work 

RL for LLM post-training and reasoning. RLHF established sequence-level post-training for aligning language models, typically using PPO-style optimization with a reference-policy regular-izer [Ouyang et al., 2022, Schulman et al., 2017]. More recent reasoning systems shift to verifiable rewards on math and code, which makes critic-free or group-based optimization practical at scale. Representative examples include GRPO, REINFORCE++, DAPO, DeepSeek-R1, Kimi k1.5, and DeepCoder [Shao et al., 2024, Hu et al., 2025, Yu et al., 2025b, Guo et al., 2025a, Kimi-Team et al., 2025, Luo et al., 2025]. Many of these systems are built around reward-maximizing or normalized-advantage objectives and have produced strong reasoning performance, but they do not explicitly target a reward-weighted response distribution and can therefore favor a small set of dominant trajectories. 16 GFlowNets and distribution matching. GFlowNets were introduced as a framework for sampling compositional objects in proportion to reward rather than greedily maximizing it [Bengio et al., 2021, 2023, Zhang et al., 2022]. Subsequent work developed stronger objectives and interpretations, includ-ing trajectory balance, a variational-inference view of GFlowNets, maximum-entropy formulations, entropy-regularized RL connections, and improved training from partial episodes [Malkin et al., 2022, 2023, Mohammadpour et al., 2024, Tiapkin et al., 2024, Madan et al., 2023], with applications extending to structured decision problems such as robust scheduling [Zhang et al., 2023]. In language modeling, amortized-inference perspectives connect GFlowNet-style objectives to reasoning over latent chains of thought [Hu et al., 2024], and closely related ideas have recently been extended to diffusion-model priors in vision, language, and control [Venkatraman et al., 2024]. The closest prior work for LLM post-training is FOR and FlowRL [Yu et al., 2025a, Zhu et al., 2026]. Our method stays in this distribution-matching line, but departs from FlowRL by removing the learned prompt-conditional partition network and replacing it with a rollout-based estimate motivated by the instability of jointly training that auxiliary model with a frontier-scale policy. Compared to reward-maximizing methods such as GRPO, the GFlowNet objective targets a reference-anchored 

distribution rather than expected reward, retaining mass on multiple high-reward reasoning paths instead of concentrating on a single mode. 

In-batch estimation and variance reduction. Our estimator is also related to a broader line of work on reducing variance in score-function and policy-gradient optimization. Classical REIN-FORCE baselines [Williams, 1992, Weaver and Tao, 2001], action-dependent baselines [Wu et al., 2018], and leave-one-out or learned control variates for variational inference and black-box gradient estimation [Richter et al., 2020, Tucker et al., 2017, Grathwohl et al., 2018] all reduce estimator noise without changing the underlying objective. Path-space log-variance objectives further provide low-variance training signals for sampling problems [Nüsken and Richter, 2021], and have been shown to coincide with the trajectory-balance loss for GFlowNets [Malkin et al., 2023], providing additional motivation for variance-aware partition estimates in our setting. In LLM RL, GRPO and REINFORCE++ stabilize critic-free updates through group-relative normalization and global advantage normalization [Shao et al., 2024, Hu et al., 2025], while DAPO emphasizes system-level stability in large-scale reasoning training [Yu et al., 2025b]. Our use of the rollout group is related in spirit to these in-batch estimators, but the quantity we estimate is different: we use the current group to estimate the partition term implied by the distribution-matching objective and inject it as a stop-gradient baseline, structurally analogous to a GRPO group-mean baseline applied to the trajectory-balance residual rather than to the advantage. 

Reasoning and safety domains. Most work on RL post-training for LLMs is evaluated on domains with verifiable rewards, especially mathematical reasoning and code generation. Representative math resources include step-level verification and olympiad-style evaluation [Lightman et al., 2023, He et al., 2024], while code evaluation commonly uses LiveCodeBench, Codeforces, and Hu-manEval+ [Jain et al., 2025, Penedo et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2021]. We additionally study LLM red-teaming, where rewards are sparser and noisier than in standard reasoning benchmarks. This setting connects our work to jailbreak benchmarks such as AdvBench and HarmBench and to recent automated attack methods including SEMA, FITD, and X-Teaming [Zou et al., 2023, Mazeika et al., 2024, Feng et al., 2026, Weng et al., 2025, Rahman et al., 2025]. We use this domain not primarily to propose a new attack method, but to test whether GFlowRL remains stable when reward feedback is substantially noisier than in math and code.

The training curves, as shown in Figure 3, further corroborate this instability: unlike GRPO and GFlowRL, whose gradient norms remain relatively smooth throughout optimization, FlowRL displays repeated, abrupt spikes spanning many orders of magnitude. These excursions are not isolated numerical artifacts but occur persistently across training, with 55 of 421 steps exceeding a gradient norm of 10 6. Such behavior indicates that the learned normalization term Zϕ destabilizes policy updates, allowing poorly calibrated estimates of log Zϕ(x) to dominate the optimization signal despite providing no measurable downstream benefit.

At the beginning of training (100 steps), GRPO achieves the best distribution fitting, as all methods are still far from convergence. While FlowRL and FlowRL-RandomLogZ produce near-random or uniform samples that do not yet capture the target structure, GFlowRL already begins to capture the underlying multimodal shape of the mixture distribution. As training progresses to 500 steps, a regime comparable to typical LLM post-training settings (e.g., math reasoning), where optimization often spans only a few hundred steps, GRPO begins to exhibit mode collapse, concentrating its probability mass on a single mode. This behavior underscores a key limitation of maximum-reward approaches: although they can rapidly identify high-reward regions, they tend to sacrifice diversity.
